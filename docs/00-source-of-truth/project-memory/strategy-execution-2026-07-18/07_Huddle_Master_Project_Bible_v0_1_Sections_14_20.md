# 14. Trust, Safety and Child Privacy

Trust is the central condition for Huddle.

Huddle asks families to use digital infrastructure to move children and parents into real-world participation. That creates a higher responsibility than a generic marketplace, directory or social platform. Parents need to feel that Huddle helps them make better decisions without exposing their children, over-collecting family data or creating unsafe contact paths.

Trust, safety and privacy must therefore be designed as product foundations, not compliance afterthoughts.

## 14.1 Strategic safety position

Huddle’s current safety position:

Huddle is designed around adult-controlled family participation. Children should not be exposed as public searchable profiles. Unknown adults should not be able to contact children directly. Family context should be used to improve relevance and participation, not to create unnecessary visibility or surveillance.

This is a design principle, not yet a legal compliance conclusion. Any final claim such as “fully compliant”, “GDPR-ready” or “safe for all children” requires expert legal/privacy/safety review.

## 14.2 Adult-controlled family participation

The first product model should place the adult at the centre of control.

The adult should manage:

- account creation;
- family context;
- child age/stage attributes;
- interests;
- approximate location;
- consent;
- communication settings;
- saved items;
- registrations or interest;
- deletion/withdrawal requests;
- block/report actions.

The child should not be made publicly searchable by default.

## 14.3 Data minimisation

Huddle should collect only what is needed to support family relevance, safety, participation and operations.

Where possible:

- use age range instead of exact birth date;
- use approximate location instead of precise home address;
- store interests separately from identity;
- avoid collecting sensitive data unless necessary;
- separate operational data from public profile data;
- avoid sharing individual family data with partners;
- keep partner reporting aggregated.

## 14.4 Organiser verification

Families need to know who is behind an activity, service or listing.

Verification can be staged:

### Basic verification

- confirmed email/phone;
- organisation name;
- profile completeness;
- terms acceptance;
- category review.

### Enhanced verification

- organisation registration;
- professional credentials where relevant;
- manual review;
- insurance or safety information where applicable;
- references or partner confirmation.

### Pilot verification

For the first Oslo pilot, verification does not need to be complex, but it must be explicit. A family should understand what has and has not been verified.

## 14.5 Communication safety

The safest first model is adult-to-organiser communication, not child-to-adult or child-to-child communication.

Communication should include:

- controlled inquiry;
- organiser response to adult;
- clear record of contact;
- report/block path;
- no direct unknown adult-to-child messaging;
- boundaries around group discussions;
- moderation for community features.

## 14.6 Marketplace safety

Children’s goods require more care than generic second-hand goods.

Marketplace safety should consider:

- product category restrictions;
- condition labels;
- prohibited items;
- recall-sensitive categories;
- seller verification;
- payment safety;
- pickup/delivery boundaries;
- refund/return clarity;
- report item/seller.

Some categories may require manual review or should be excluded early.

## 14.7 Experts and service safety

The experts layer requires additional caution.

Huddle must avoid:

- unverified professional claims;
- medical or therapeutic outcome promises;
- unclear emergency boundaries;
- unsafe advice;
- hidden sponsored recommendations;
- weak credential display.

Before launch, the experts layer needs:

- credential policy;
- category rules;
- professional disclaimers;
- moderation/reporting;
- privacy review;
- legal review.

## 14.8 Incident handling

The MVP must have a basic incident process before pilot.

At minimum:

- report intake;
- severity classification;
- owner assignment;
- escalation path;
- response template;
- evidence log;
- follow-up status;
- partner notification rule where relevant;
- legal/privacy escalation rule.

## 14.9 Public partner data boundaries

Municipalities and partners should receive aggregated insights only.

They should not receive:

- individual child profiles;
- sensitive family-level dossiers;
- unrestricted exports;
- identifiable behavioural data;
- health/development data unless explicitly and legally governed.

Huddle’s public-value credibility depends on this boundary.

## 14.10 Trust & Safety owner

Trust and safety need a named owner before MVP/pilot.

This can initially be an outsourced advisor or part-time expert, but the role must exist before:

- DPIA baseline;
- pilot partner discussions involving child/family data;
- launch of community features;
- launch of expert/service features;
- public compliance claims.

# 15. Business Model

Huddle’s business model should be staged behind proof of family value.

The project should not begin by maximising short-term monetisation. If early families, organisers and partners do not experience practical value, no revenue model will be credible. The first commercial goal is to validate a repeatable participation unit in Oslo.

## 15.1 Business model thesis

Huddle can become a multi-sided family participation platform with revenue from:

- organiser tools;
- activity/event transactions;
- marketplace and rental fees;
- partner commerce;
- sponsored but clearly labelled family campaigns;
- expert/service commissions;
- municipal/public-value pilots;
- B2B partner services;
- future premium tools and integrations.

These are planned revenue layers, not proven revenue.

## 15.2 Revenue layer 1 — Organizer tools

Organizers may pay for tools if Huddle helps them:

- reach relevant families;
- manage listings;
- reduce repeated questions;
- handle capacity;
- communicate updates;
- receive better-fit inquiries;
- understand participation patterns.

Potential model:

- free/basic listings during pilot;
- low-cost subscription for enhanced organiser tools;
- higher-tier tools for repeated organisers later.

## 15.3 Revenue layer 2 — Activity and event fees

Huddle may take a small fee or commission when paid activities are booked through the platform.

This depends on the booking model:

- internal booking;
- external booking handoff;
- registration interest;
- partner payment system.

The first MVP must close this decision before detailed financial assumptions are made.

## 15.4 Revenue layer 3 — Marketplace and rental

Marketplace revenue may come from:

- transaction commission;
- listing fees later;
- partner-store sales;
- rental fees;
- verified seller tools;
- promoted collections.

Early commission should be modest to encourage supply and adoption.

## 15.5 Revenue layer 4 — Partner stores and brands

Partner commerce may include:

- sponsored collections;
- seasonal campaigns;
- affiliate/revenue share;
- product feed integration;
- premium placement;
- partner store profiles;
- API integration fees later.

All sponsored content must be clearly labelled.

## 15.6 Revenue layer 5 — Experts and services

Experts and family services may support:

- commission on bookings;
- profile subscription;
- lead fee;
- curated programmes;
- group sessions;
- content partnerships.

This layer requires stronger credential and legal review.

## 15.7 Revenue layer 6 — Municipal and public-value pilots

Municipalities and public partners may fund:

- pilot implementation;
- local participation mapping;
- programme visibility;
- inclusion initiatives;
- evaluation;
- privacy-safe reporting.

Huddle should treat public-value revenue carefully. It must not compromise family trust or create data incentives that conflict with privacy.

## 15.8 Funding and capital strategy

Current planning assumption: a staged NOK 20 million foundation round combining investor capital, grants, strategic partners and a possible second close.

This must remain labelled as a planning assumption until reconciled with the current financial model and founder-approved.

The funding logic is evidence-gated:

- early validation;
- MVP and trust foundation;
- Oslo controlled launch;
- Norway city-cell proof;
- seed/scale conversations.

## 15.9 What not to claim yet

Do not claim:

- proven revenue;
- confirmed commission rates;
- confirmed partner contracts;
- measured public impact;
- national scalability;
- full compliance;
- product-market fit.

Use:

- planned;
- designed to;
- hypothesis;
- pilot;
- validation;
- evidence-gated.

# 16. Oslo-first Launch Strategy

Huddle should start in Oslo because the project needs local density, not broad but shallow reach.

A family participation platform becomes useful when enough relevant families, organisers, activities, products, services and public partners exist in the same local context. A national launch without density would risk becoming another directory.

## 16.1 City-cell strategy

The city-cell model means selecting a bounded local area where Huddle can test:

- family demand;
- organiser supply;
- trusted discovery;
- adult-controlled contact;
- activity attendance;
- repeat participation;
- marketplace/reuse relevance;
- public-value partner interest;
- trust-and-safety operations.

The first city-cell should be small enough to manage and dense enough to produce learning.

## 16.2 Why Oslo

Oslo is the logical first test because:

- it has diverse family segments;
- it has high activity and institutional density;
- it contains public, private and voluntary family infrastructure;
- it offers a meaningful municipality/public-value context;
- it can reveal access, language, cost and local-network barriers;
- it can support an evidence-rich pilot.

## 16.3 Candidate area selection

The first validation phase should identify one to three candidate Oslo neighbourhoods.

Selection criteria:

- family density;
- diversity of family needs;
- organiser/activity supply;
- public partner relevance;
- transport accessibility;
- existing local programmes;
- cost/access barriers;
- operational feasibility;
- safety support capacity;
- ability to measure participation.

## 16.4 Launch sequencing

### Stage 1 — Market clarification

Goal:

Validate family, organiser, municipality and trust assumptions.

Outputs:

- interviews;
- partner conversations;
- first source/evidence pack;
- pilot geography shortlist;
- MVP scope freeze.

### Stage 2 — MVP and trust foundation

Goal:

Build the minimum product and safety operations needed to test the participation chain.

Outputs:

- MVP;
- preliminary DPIA/trust baseline;
- organiser onboarding;
- design system;
- controlled beta.

### Stage 3 — Oslo controlled pilot

Goal:

Run a bounded pilot with real families and organisers.

Outputs:

- participation data;
- repeat-use evidence;
- incident/support learnings;
- partner feedback;
- public-value report.

### Stage 4 — Norway city-cell proof

Goal:

Prepare repeatability beyond the first city-cell.

Outputs:

- city playbook;
- second-city candidate;
- refined business model;
- seed/investor readiness.

# 17. 90-Day Validation Plan

The first 90 days should replace assumptions with decision-quality evidence.

The purpose is not to prove everything. The purpose is to decide whether Huddle should proceed into MVP build and Oslo pilot preparation, and if yes, with what scope.

## 17.1 Day 90 decision

At day 90, Huddle should make one of three decisions:

### GO

Evidence supports controlled MVP build and pilot preparation.

### ITERATE

There is meaningful signal, but the segment, scope, trust model, geography or business layer must change.

### STOP-PAUSE

The problem, willingness to use, organiser supply, safety model or public-value pathway is not strong enough without a major rethink.

## 17.2 Workstream A — Families

Target:

40–60 structured parent/caregiver interviews.

Segments:

- parents of younger children;
- parents of school-age children;
- parents of teenagers;
- recently moved families;
- multilingual families;
- lower-income households;
- single-parent or shared-care households;
- families with accessibility or sensory requirements;
- parents with limited local networks.

Questions:

- How do families currently find activities, support and equipment?
- Where does the process break?
- What information is missing?
- What creates trust?
- What prevents participation?
- What would make them return?
- What data would they be willing to share?
- What would they refuse?

## 17.3 Workstream B — Organisers and supply

Target:

20–30 conversations.

Segments:

- sports clubs;
- creative studios;
- libraries;
- museums;
- independent instructors;
- voluntary organisations;
- family services;
- retailers and equipment partners.

Questions:

- Will they maintain accurate listings?
- What data can they provide?
- What capacity and schedule issues exist?
- What acquisition problems do they have?
- Would they join a pilot?
- What commercial model would be acceptable?
- What would block participation?

## 17.4 Workstream C — Public-value partners

Target:

10–20 conversations.

Segments:

- municipal contacts;
- community organisations;
- inclusion organisations;
- youth/family programmes;
- evaluation partners.

Questions:

- What local participation problems matter?
- Which families are hardest to reach?
- Which programmes need visibility?
- What privacy boundaries are required?
- What would make a pilot useful?
- Is there funding/co-funding potential?

## 17.5 Workstream D — Trust, privacy and safety

Validate:

- adult account model;
- no public child profiles;
- organiser verification;
- consent capture;
- deletion/withdrawal;
- controlled contact;
- incident reporting;
- community boundaries;
- aggregated reporting.

Output:

- preliminary DPIA/safety baseline;
- open legal/privacy questions;
- minimum trust operations plan.

## 17.6 Workstream E — MVP scope freeze

By day 90, decide:

- adult verification level;
- internal vs external booking;
- whether family-to-family connect is in MVP;
- first reuse/equipment category;
- North Star metric;
- organiser verification threshold;
- partner reporting boundaries.

## 17.7 Day 90 outputs

Required:

- research repository;
- interview synthesis;
- segment ranking;
- organiser pipeline;
- public-value partner notes;
- city-cell recommendation;
- MVP scope;
- risk register;
- updated claims;
- GO / ITERATE / STOP-PAUSE memo.

# 19. Technical Direction

The technical system should be designed to support trust, privacy, local density and staged growth.

The first objective is not technical completeness. It is building a reliable MVP that can test the participation chain safely.

## 19.1 Core product domains

Initial domains:

- adult user account;
- family profile/context;
- child context attributes;
- consent;
- activities/events;
- organisers;
- listings;
- saved items;
- registration interest/booking handoff;
- notifications;
- reports/incidents;
- operations console;
- aggregated pilot metrics.

Later domains:

- marketplace;
- product catalogue;
- partner stores;
- expert services;
- community groups;
- payment;
- API integrations;
- municipality dashboard;
- advanced recommendations.

## 19.2 Architecture principles

### Privacy-aware by default

The data model must minimise child exposure and avoid unnecessary sensitive data.

### Role-based access

Different users need different access:

- parent/adult;
- organiser;
- expert;
- partner store;
- Huddle operations;
- municipal/pilot partner;
- admin.

### Auditability

Operations should be able to review:

- reports;
- consent changes;
- deletion requests;
- organiser changes;
- incident status;
- partner exports.

### Modular growth

Marketplace, community, services and municipality layers should not be tightly coupled before they are validated.

## 19.3 MVP technical scope

Must support:

- sign-up/sign-in;
- adult account;
- protected family context;
- activity search/browse;
- activity detail;
- organiser profile;
- saved activity;
- action path;
- reminders/notifications;
- basic operations console;
- report/block/support;
- aggregated pilot metrics.

## 19.4 Integration strategy

Do not build complex external API integrations too early.

Stage integrations:

1. Manual/CSV/import for pilot content.
2. Lightweight partner upload.
3. Product feed/import for partner stores.
4. API for inventory/catalogue.
5. Booking/payment integrations.
6. Municipality/reporting exports with strict privacy boundaries.

## 19.5 Security and compliance direction

Requires human expert review.

Early requirements:

- secure authentication;
- least-privilege admin access;
- data minimisation;
- consent records;
- deletion/export request process;
- incident logging;
- privacy policy alignment;
- clear data retention approach;
- secure storage;
- audit trail.

## 19.6 Technical documentation required next

After MVP decisions are closed:

- Technical Specification v1;
- data model;
- user roles and permissions;
- API assumptions;
- privacy impact checklist;
- security baseline;
- integration roadmap;
- analytics/measurement plan;
- QA/test plan.

# 20. Brand and Design System

The final Huddle Brand Guidelines are the current brand source of truth.

The brand is not a decorative layer added after product decisions. It is part of trust. Families, municipalities, experts, organisers, partner stores and investors should all encounter one coherent Huddle identity: warm, premium, calm, human, safe and locally grounded.

## 20.1 Source of truth

Use only:

- approved Huddle Family logo;
- approved icon/mark;
- approved colour palette;
- approved brand direction;
- final 42-page brand guidelines package;
- current approved Canva/Figma/PDF/PPTX exports.

Do not use:

- old logos;
- old colour directions;
- black cover versions;
- broken Figma reconstructions;
- low-quality image slices;
- flat PNGs as editable design.

## 20.2 Brand role in product

The brand must communicate:

- trust;
- warmth;
- family safety;
- premium calm;
- local belonging;
- sustainable choices;
- clarity;
- human care.

It should not feel:

- childish;
- corporate-cold;
- medical;
- chaotic;
- manipulative;
- over-social;
- generic marketplace-like.

## 20.3 Design system

The design system should include:

- colour tokens;
- typography;
- spacing;
- buttons;
- forms;
- cards;
- navigation;
- filters;
- badges;
- status labels;
- organiser cards;
- activity cards;
- marketplace cards;
- expert cards;
- trust/safety components;
- empty states;
- error states;
- notification patterns;
- dashboard components.

## 20.4 Website and product are separate

Huddle needs separate design tracks:

### Brandbook

Final identity and brand rules.

### Public website

Explains the project, collects waitlist interest and supports external trust.

### MVP product

Lets families and organisers complete the participation chain.

### Investor/grant decks

Translate the project into funder-specific arguments.

Mixing these creates confusion. They can share the same brand system, but they are not the same artefact.

## 20.5 Reference images

The existing large set of flat website screens and generated references may be useful for direction, but they are not production design.

Use them for:

- layout inspiration;
- content hierarchy;
- category ideas;
- tone;
- section mapping.

Do not use them as:

- final editable Figma;
- final product UI;
- approved brand assets;
- production-ready components.

## 20.6 Award-level ambition

Huddle can aim for high visual quality, but award-level design must not come at the expense of:

- usability;
- accessibility;
- trust;
- conversion;
- product clarity;
- development feasibility;
- privacy and safety.

The right target is not visual spectacle. It is a premium, emotionally intelligent family product that feels calmer and more trustworthy than the alternatives.

## 20.7 Design next steps

After Master Project Bible and MVP scope:

1. Extract final design tokens from the brandbook.
2. Build Figma component library.
3. Define public website information architecture.
4. Define MVP user flows.
5. Design priority screens only.
6. Conduct brand/design QA.
7. Prepare developer handoff.
