Huddle Family

Trusted family life and participation infrastructure.

## Slide 2 — The world families navigate today

Title:

Family life is digitally crowded, but locally fragmented.

Main message:

Parents are surrounded by information, but the useful answers are scattered.

Suggested copy:

Activities, groups, services, goods, experts and public programmes exist — but families often have to search across disconnected pages, chats, marketplaces, calendars and recommendations to understand what actually fits.

Visual direction:

Fragmented map of channels converging toward one family.

## Slide 3 — The parent’s daily question

Title:

What can we do nearby, safely and with people we can trust?

Main message:

This is the practical question Huddle is built around.

Suggested copy:

Families do not only need more options. They need clarity: what fits this child, this schedule, this budget, this location and this level of trust.

Visual direction:

Parent with child, surrounded by calm decision cards.

## Slide 4 — Discovery friction becomes participation friction

Title:

When discovery breaks, participation breaks.

Main message:

Opportunities can exist locally and still remain out of reach.

Suggested copy:

A child can miss an activity not because it does not exist, but because the family never found it, did not trust it, could not understand the requirements, could not afford the equipment, or missed the right moment to join.

Visual direction:

Journey line with barriers: find → understand → trust → join → return.

# Section B — Problem

## Slide 6 — Fragmentation is the problem

Title:

The issue is not lack of information.

Main message:

The issue is lack of trusted family context.

Suggested copy:

Cities already contain activities, public programmes, clubs, experts, communities and useful goods. But they rarely function as one understandable family ecosystem.

Visual direction:

Many scattered tiles becoming one structured Huddle interface.

## Slide 7 — What parents need before they act

Title:

Parents need decision-quality answers.

Main message:

A listing is not enough.

Suggested copy:

Is it age-appropriate? Is it close? Is there space? What does it cost? Is equipment needed? Who runs it? Can we trust it? What happens after first contact?

Visual direction:

Checklist around an activity card.

## Slide 8 — Unequal burden

Title:

The burden is not equal.

Main message:

Families with less time, money, language access or local network search harder and miss more.

Suggested copy:

The families who most need support may be the least able to navigate fragmented systems.

Visual direction:

Split visual: easy path vs complex path.

## Slide 9 — Digital childhood context

Title:

Digital access starts early.

Main message:

Families need positive real-world alternatives, not only restrictions.

Suggested copy:

Norwegian children gain independent digital access early, and concerns around social media and adolescent wellbeing are increasing. Huddle’s position is that digital policy needs a positive local layer.

Claim IDs:

C-011, C-012, C-027.

Visual direction:

Phone/digital dots contrasted with real-world activity/community.

## Slide 10 — Cost and equipment barriers

Title:

Participation is also practical.

Main message:

Cost, equipment, travel and access shape whether children can join.

Suggested copy:

When participation requires gear, transport, fees or insider knowledge, families need clearer pathways to free, subsidised, reused or lower-cost options.

Claim IDs:

C-013, C-014, C-026.

Visual direction:

Activity card connected to equipment/reuse/support options.
