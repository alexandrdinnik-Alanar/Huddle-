## Executive summary

Huddle is designed as trusted family participation infrastructure, starting with one controlled Oslo city-cell before broader build-out. `[C-001]` `[C-006]` `[C-010]`

The purpose of the Oslo city-cell pilot is not to prove national scale. The purpose is to test whether a bounded local ecosystem can move families from fragmented digital discovery to safe, relevant and repeated real-world participation.

The pilot is built around one practical question:

Can Huddle create enough family demand, organiser supply, trust and operational control in one Oslo city-cell to justify MVP build, continued funding and later expansion?

The proposal should be read as a pilot design hypothesis, not as evidence of completed validation. Huddle is currently pre-validation. `[C-001]`

## What a city-cell means

A city-cell is the smallest local geography where Huddle can test the complete participation chain:

Family context → discover → understand → trust → act → attend → return

It is not a marketing area. It is an operating unit with enough density to test whether Huddle can create useful local behaviour.

A credible first city-cell needs:

- reachable family population;
- relevant activities, services, community groups and family resources;
- organisers willing to maintain accurate supply data;
- a mix of free, subsidised and paid options;
- practical transport and neighbourhood access;
- potential public or community entry points;
- manageable support and safety operations;
- privacy-safe measurement boundaries.

## Pilot decision question

The pilot should produce a clear recommendation:

- GO — evidence supports controlled MVP build and pilot continuation;
- ITERATE — signal exists, but the segment, offer, geography, trust model or product scope must change;
- STOP-PAUSE — the assumptions are not strong enough to continue without a major rethink.

The goal is decision-quality evidence, not optimistic storytelling.

## Pilot objectives

The pilot is designed to test:

1. Whether the family problem is frequent and painful enough to create repeat use.
2. Which family segments feel the strongest need.
3. Whether structured family context improves relevance.
4. Whether adult-controlled trust and safety boundaries increase willingness to act. `[C-015]`
5. Whether organisers will provide and maintain useful activity, capacity, price and age-fit information.
6. Whether families progress from discovery to action, attendance and return.
7. Whether cost, equipment, reuse or rental visibility reduces participation friction.
8. Whether support, moderation and incident handling can operate safely at small scale.
9. Whether public partners find aggregated, privacy-safe insight useful. `[C-019]`
10. Whether the first city-cell can become a repeatable operating playbook.

## Proposed pilot path

### Phase 0 — 90-day validation before pilot

Status: planned validation programme. `[C-007]` `[C-008]` `[C-009]`

Purpose: decide whether Huddle should proceed into MVP build and city-cell preparation.

Core work:

- 40–60 parent/caregiver interviews;
- 20–30 organiser and supply conversations;
- 10–20 municipality, NGO or public-value partner conversations;
- trust, privacy and safety model testing;
- candidate neighbourhood shortlist;
- first MVP scope freeze;
- preliminary privacy and Trust & Safety baseline;
- pilot budget and measurement reconciliation.

Outputs:

- coded family problem evidence;
- organiser/supply readiness map;
- public-value partner signal;
- one to three candidate Oslo neighbourhoods;
- MVP scope decision;
- preliminary risk register;
- GO / ITERATE / STOP-PAUSE memo.

### Phase 1 — City-cell preparation

Indicative duration: 6–8 weeks after Phase 0 GO decision.

Core work:

- select the first city-cell geography;
- appoint a city-cell operations owner;
- appoint Trust & Safety and privacy review owners;
- onboard first organisers and public/community programmes;
- structure activity, price, capacity, age-fit and accessibility data;
- recruit a controlled family cohort;
- prepare support, escalation and incident routines;
- establish baseline measures;
- complete readiness review.

Exit criteria:

- initial organiser supply is live and data-reviewed;
- family cohort is recruited through appropriate consent and onboarding;
- support and safety routines are operational;
- measurement plan is approved;
- partner reporting boundaries are documented.

### Phase 2 — Controlled family pilot

Indicative duration: 8–12 weeks after readiness approval.

Core work:

- release discovery and participation flows to a limited cohort;
- monitor discovery, relevance, abandonment and support requests;
- test adult-controlled contact and action paths;
- verify attendance where possible;
- test reminders, updates and return loops;
- operate weekly data-quality and Trust & Safety reviews;
- collect family and organiser feedback after real participation.

Exit criteria:

- families complete the participation chain;
- organiser information remains usable;
- support burden is understood;
- trust and privacy friction is measured;
- partner reporting works without exposing individual child or family data;
- decision memo recommends scale, iteration or pause.

### Phase 3 — Repeat and ecosystem test

Indicative duration: 8–12 weeks after Phase 2 review.

Core work:

- test repeat participation;
- test recurring groups or recurring organiser relationships where relevant;
- evaluate cost/equipment/reuse friction;
- assess organiser continuation and operational load;
- compare density across neighbourhood segments;
- prepare second-city-cell playbook.

Exit criteria:

- repeat behaviour is observed or clearly not observed;
- strongest family segment is identified;
- strongest organiser category is identified;
- cost and operating assumptions are updated;
- recommendation is made for second city-cell, iteration or pause.

## Candidate city-cell selection criteria

The first city-cell should be selected against practical operating criteria, not only demographic attractiveness.

| Criterion | What must be tested | Required decision before pilot |
|---|---|---|
| Family density | Enough families to test discovery, action and return | Candidate neighbourhood shortlist |
| Supply density | Enough organisers, activities and useful local resources | First organiser pipeline |
| Inclusion relevance | Real barriers around cost, access, language, equipment or network gaps | Public-value hypothesis fit |
| Trust feasibility | Families and organisers can understand safety boundaries | Trust model baseline |
| Operational feasibility | Support, moderation and updates can be handled manually if needed | Operations owner and routines |
| Public/community route | Potential partner entry points exist | Partner outreach list |
| Measurement feasibility | Results can be measured without exposing sensitive family data | Measurement plan |

## Stakeholders

### Families

Families are the primary user group. The pilot should include families across different stages and needs, including:

- parents of babies and toddlers;
- parents of preschool and school-age children;
- parents of adolescents;
- recently moved families;
- multilingual families;
- families with lower budgets;
- single-parent or shared-care households;
- families with accessibility or sensory requirements.

The sample is for problem discovery and product learning. It should not be presented as population-level statistical proof.

### Organisers and supply partners

The first supply base may include:

- activity providers;
- sports clubs and coaches;
- libraries, museums and culture venues;
- children’s classes and studios;
- voluntary organisations;
- family-facing service providers;
- children’s goods, reuse, rental or equipment partners.

Organiser readiness should be judged by data quality, response time, willingness to maintain listings and ability to handle family inquiries safely.

### Municipalities and public-value partners

Public partners may help with:

- understanding local participation barriers;
- identifying relevant public programmes;
- advising on inclusion and family support priorities;
- shaping privacy-safe measurement;
- connecting Huddle to trusted community actors;
- evaluating whether the pilot produces useful public insight.

Public partners must not receive individual child profiles, individual family dossiers or unrestricted platform access.

### NGO and community partners

NGO/community partners may support:

- trust-building with families;
- recruitment of under-served segments;
- inclusion and accessibility feedback;
- programme visibility;
- grant or co-funding routes where an ordinary AS is not the best applicant route. `[C-025]`

## Measurement framework

The pilot should measure signals that support a decision, not vanity metrics.

| Area | Example signals | Evidence boundary |
|---|---|---|
| Family demand | Problem frequency, current workaround, stated urgency | Interviews are signal, not proof |
| Relevance | Whether families understand why recommendations fit | Requires prototype or MVP testing |
| Trust | Consent friction, willingness to create family context, safety objections | Requires privacy/legal review |
| Action | Contact, registration, booking or save-to-plan completion | MVP/pilot evidence |
| Attendance | Confirmed attendance where possible | Pilot evidence |
| Return | Repeat discovery or repeat participation | Pilot evidence |
| Organiser value | Data maintenance, inbound quality, continuation willingness | Pilot evidence |
| Cost/access barriers | Price, equipment, transport, schedule, accessibility | Mixed qualitative and pilot evidence |
| Support load | Support requests, incident volume and escalation | Operations evidence |
| Public value | Usefulness of aggregated local insight | Partner review and pilot evidence |

Huddle should not claim measured public impact before pilot evidence exists. `[C-020]`

## Privacy and child-safety boundaries

The pilot must remain adult-controlled and privacy-first.

Current product principles:

- adults manage family participation;
- children should not have public searchable profiles by default; `[C-017]`
- unknown adults should not contact children directly;
- family context should be limited to what is needed for fit and participation;
- partner reporting should be aggregated and purpose-defined;
- sensitive data should not be exposed in investor, grant or partner data rooms;
- legal/privacy review is required before claiming compliance or GDPR readiness. `[C-016]`

Potential partner reporting may include:

- demand by activity category;
- free/subsidised demand;
- cost and equipment barriers;
- discovery-to-action conversion at aggregate level;
- attendance and return patterns at aggregate level;
- organiser data-quality indicators;
- support/incident categories at aggregate level.

It should not include:

- individual child profiles;
- identifiable family-level behavioural trails;
- sensitive child or family dossiers;
- unrestricted access dashboards;
- secondary use of family data without clear consent and review.

## What Huddle provides

Huddle’s pilot contribution is the operating system around the participation chain:

- structured family discovery;
- organiser/category information model;
- TrustMatch-style fit explanation;
- adult-controlled action paths;
- safety and support routines;
- local partner onboarding;
- pilot measurement model;
- aggregated reporting design;
- evidence and decision memo.

## What partners may provide

Depending on partner type, pilot partners may provide:

- local context and programme mapping;
- introductions to organisers and community actors;
- recruitment channels for family interviews;
- inclusion/accessibility feedback;
- privacy-safe evaluation input;
- co-funding or grant-route participation;
- pilot review participation.

No partner obligation should be implied until a formal agreement exists.
