# 21. Team and Operating Model

Huddle is an ambitious multi-layer project. It cannot be managed as a loose collection of documents, designs, grant ideas and development tasks. It needs a clear operating model: who decides, who drafts, who reviews, who approves, and which work must be handled by humans rather than AI.

The current reality should be stated honestly: Huddle does not yet have a full hired team. Many roles are planned, needed or outsource-first. AI agents can accelerate research, drafting, QA and operations, but they cannot replace founder decisions, legal accountability, privacy judgement, financial responsibility or human partner relationships.

## 21.1 Founder / CEO

Current owner: Alexander.

Responsibilities:

- project vision;
- final strategy;
- approval of external documents;
- funding and investor decisions;
- company/legal decisions;
- hiring decisions;
- brand approval;
- partner commitments;
- prioritisation.

Cannot be delegated:

- external commitments;
- legal claims;
- financial promises;
- investor promises;
- final founder approval.

## 21.2 Critical outsourced layer

These roles should be involved early, even if part-time or advisory.

### Privacy / Child Safety / Legal Advisor

Why it matters:

Huddle involves families, children, adult-controlled accounts, privacy, community, marketplace, experts and public-sector relationships. This requires professional review.

First tasks:

- review safety principles;
- review child/privacy claims;
- review MVP data assumptions;
- define legal review points;
- support DPIA baseline planning.

### Grant / Funding Advisor

Why it matters:

Grant eligibility, applicant route and wording can decide whether an application is credible.

First tasks:

- verify Wave 1 grants;
- separate AS route from NGO/municipality partner route;
- review grant narrative;
- create application checklists.

### Finance Controller

Why it matters:

Investor and grant documents must not contain unsupported financial assumptions.

First tasks:

- reconcile NOK 20m planning assumption;
- update Norway/Oslo cost model;
- add marketing/events/travel costs;
- model B2B commission;
- prepare use-of-funds.

### Product Manager

Why it matters:

Huddle can easily overbuild. Product leadership is needed to protect MVP scope.

First tasks:

- close MVP open decisions;
- define acceptance criteria;
- align design and technical work;
- prevent scope overload.

### Tech Lead / CTO-track

Why it matters:

The product must be technically credible, privacy-aware and buildable.

First tasks:

- review technical assumptions;
- define architecture direction;
- review security baseline;
- prepare MVP technical spec.

## 21.4 AI-agent layer

AI agents should support:

- contact external people without approval;
- make legal decisions;
- approve financial assumptions;

- mark external documents final without QA.

# 22. Funding Strategy

Huddle should combine several funding routes rather than depend on one path.

The project has public-value logic, commercial potential, innovation potential and social participation relevance. That gives Huddle several funding categories, but each category requires different framing and eligibility checks.

## 22.1 Funding principles

### Evidence before scale

Funding should support validation, MVP, trust foundation and pilot evidence before broad expansion.

### Do not use one generic application

Investor, grant, municipality, NGO, corporate and crowdfunding materials require different language.

### Applicant route must be checked

Some routes may support an ordinary Norwegian AS directly. Others may require NGO, public-sector or partner route. Huddle must verify this before applying.

### Public value should not weaken commercial clarity

Huddle can have social value and still be a scalable company. The documents should make this relationship clear rather than apologetic.

# 23. Investor Case

The investor case for Huddle should not be framed as “a website for parents”.

The stronger thesis is that Huddle can become a trusted family participation infrastructure layer. It sits between family life, local discovery, community, marketplace, services, public programmes and trusted commerce.

## 23.1 Investor thesis

Huddle is building a category around trusted family participation.

If the Oslo city-cell model works, Huddle can expand across:

- activities and events;
- family commerce;
- pre-owned/reuse economy;
- local experts;
- organiser tools;
- family communities;
- public-value pilots;
- partner brands;
- municipality relationships.

The commercial opportunity grows because Huddle can own trusted family context, not just traffic.

## 23.2 Why now

Several forces make the timing relevant:

- family discovery is fragmented;
- digital childhood concerns are increasing;
- municipalities and public partners need better participation tools;
- parents need safer alternatives to chaotic social groups;
- sustainable family commerce is increasingly relevant;
- local businesses need more trusted family acquisition channels;
- AI and modern product systems can improve matching and coordination if used transparently and safely.

## 23.3 Why Huddle can differentiate

Differentiation:

- family-first rather than generic marketplace;
- adult-controlled child context;
- local participation chain;
- trust and privacy as product layer;
- marketplace connected to real family need;
- public-value and commercial logic together;
- Oslo city-cell proof before broad scale;
- evidence-gated development.

## 23.4 First proof

The first proof is not revenue at national scale.

The first proof is:

- families confirm painful repeat problem;
- organisers provide usable supply;
- parents trust the product enough to act;
- families return after first use;
- safety operations are manageable;
- public-value partners see credible pilot value;
- unit of local density can be repeated.

## 23.5 Fundraising logic

The current planning model is a staged NOK 20 million foundation round. This remains a planning assumption until finance review and founder approval.

Use of funds should support:

- validation;
- MVP and mobile delivery;
- trust and safety;
- privacy/legal work;
- product design;
- organiser onboarding;
- Oslo pilot operations;
- evidence production;
- core team.

## 23.6 Investor risk framing

Professional investor materials should openly show risks:

- marketplace liquidity;
- trust and safety complexity;
- privacy/legal risk;
- organiser data quality;
- family adoption;
- MVP scope overload;
- funding route dependency;
- operational intensity of local pilots.

The case is stronger when risks are named and controlled, not hidden.

# 24. Risks and Assumptions

Huddle should maintain a living risk register.

The project’s ambition is large, and that creates real risks. The correct response is not to simplify the story into something false. The correct response is to sequence the work carefully and label assumptions honestly.

## 24.1 Product risk

Risk:

Families may like the concept but not use it repeatedly.

Control:

- 90-day validation;
- MVP participation chain;
- repeat participation metrics;
- narrow city-cell.

## 24.2 Supply risk

Risk:

Organisers may not maintain accurate listings or may not see enough value.

Control:

- organiser interviews;
- manual onboarding first;
- simple listing tools;
- supply readiness ranking.

## 24.3 Trust and safety risk

Risk:

Family and child-related platform trust can be damaged quickly if safety systems are weak.

Control:

- adult-controlled model;
- no public child profiles;
- named Trust & Safety owner;
- incident process;
- expert review before launch.

## 24.4 Privacy/legal risk

Risk:

Child/family data, public partner reporting, expert services and marketplace flows create legal/privacy complexity.

Control:

- data minimisation;
- privacy review;
- DPIA baseline;
- aggregated reporting only;
- no compliance claims before review.

## 24.5 Scope overload risk

Risk:

Trying to build marketplace, community, experts, municipality dashboard, B2B API and full MVP at once will slow or break the project.

Control:

- one participation chain first;
- staged roadmap;
- explicit out-of-MVP list;
- product manager/tech lead review.

## 24.6 Financial risk

Risk:

Costs in Norway/Oslo may be higher than early assumptions.

Control:

- Norway-specific cost model;
- finance controller review;
- conservative revenue assumptions;
- staged hiring;
- grants and partner funding.

## 24.7 Funding risk

Risk:

Some grant routes may not fit an ordinary AS, and investor fundraising may take longer than expected.

Control:

- funding route classification;
- NGO/municipality partner route where appropriate;
- multiple funding categories;
- evidence-based materials.

## 24.8 Design risk

Risk:

The project may lose time trying to convert non-editable reference screens into production design.

Control:

- final brand source of truth;
- new editable Figma system;
- reference screens used only as reference;
- art director QA.

## 24.9 Public-value risk

Risk:

Huddle could overclaim impact before evidence exists.

Control:

- public-value hypothesis language;
- pilot measurement;
- claims register;
- no “measured impact” before pilot.

## 24.10 Strategic assumption register

Current assumptions:

- Oslo is the correct first city-cell environment.
- Parents will value trusted fit enough to return.
- Organisers will participate if onboarding is low-friction.
- Public partners will see value in aggregated insight.
- Marketplace/reuse can reduce participation friction.
- Partner stores will prefer contextual family reach over generic ads.
- A staged funding model can support validation before scale.

Each assumption must be tested or clearly labelled.

## Appendix I — Glossary

Recommended terms:

### Family participation infrastructure

The trusted system that helps families discover, evaluate, trust and participate in local activities, services, community and commerce.

### City-cell

A bounded local launch area with enough family demand, organizer supply and operational focus to validate repeat participation.

### Adult-controlled family context

Family information managed by an authorised adult, designed to improve relevance without exposing children publicly.

### Evidence-gated growth

A growth approach where each stage unlocks only after decision-quality evidence is produced.

### Public-value hypothesis

The unproven but testable claim that Huddle can help public partners reduce participation barriers and improve family access through a privacy-first model.

### TrustMatch

The product concept that explains why an activity, service, product or programme may fit a family context in clear, transparent terms.
