## Executive summary

Families often live close to activities, institutions, local programmes, community groups and useful resources, yet still struggle to participate.

The challenge is not simply lack of supply. Information is spread across municipal pages, club websites, social groups, private chats, registration systems, marketplaces and word-of-mouth recommendations. Price, availability, age fit, accessibility, equipment requirements and trust indicators are often incomplete or difficult to compare.

This creates practical and trust barriers between families and the opportunities around them.

Huddle is designed as a trusted digital doorway into local family life. It helps adults discover relevant activities, programmes, organisers, communities, family services and children’s goods; understand why an option may fit; make controlled first contact; and move toward actual participation.

The public-value hypothesis is specific:

A privacy-first family participation platform may help more families discover, attend and repeat suitable local activities by reducing practical, informational and trust barriers. `[C-019]` `[C-020]`

The proposed next step is not broad deployment. It is a controlled Oslo city-cell validation and pilot path that produces decision-quality evidence before scale. `[C-001]` `[C-006]` `[C-010]`

## Public problem

### 1. Participation opportunities exist, but access is unequal

Norway has substantial activity and public-support infrastructure, yet families do not access it equally.

Statistics Norway reporting indicates that organised sports participation varies by age and income, and that equipment/travel costs are a barrier for many families. `[C-013]` `[C-014]` `[C-026]`

The challenge is not only whether activities exist. It is whether families can find, trust, afford and join them.

### 2. Digital childhood needs positive local alternatives

Norwegian children gain independent digital access early. Medietilsynet’s 2024 reporting states that 94% of children aged 9–11 had their own mobile phone, and its public reporting also notes widespread social media use among children below formal platform age thresholds. `[C-011]` `[C-027]`

WHO Europe reported that problematic social-media use among adolescents in the WHO European region increased from 7% in 2018 to 11% in 2022. `[C-012]`

Restrictions and safeguards can reduce some harms, but they do not create friendships, transport, equipment, free places, trusted organisers or suitable local activities.

Huddle addresses the practical public question that follows:

What can families do instead — nearby, safely, affordably and with people they can trust?

### 3. Fragmented discovery weakens inclusion

Families with strong local networks, more time, higher budgets and better language access can often navigate fragmented information. Families without those advantages may miss opportunities that are technically available but practically hard to reach.

This creates exclusion by friction.

For public partners, this matters because participation can support:

- belonging;
- confidence;
- social development;
- physical activity;
- cultural access;
- community connection;
- family support;
- better visibility of public programmes.

## Proposed intervention

Huddle connects one participation chain:

Family context → discover → understand → trust → act → attend → return

The platform is designed around seven capabilities:

1. Discover — relevant activities, programmes, groups, services and goods nearby.
2. TrustMatch — clear reasons why an option may fit a family context.
3. Connect — adult-controlled contact around a specific activity or local need.
4. Do — registration, reminders, updates and attendance support.
5. Belong — recurring groups, local relationships and parent-led community.
6. Equip — reuse, rental and trusted commerce that can reduce participation friction.
7. Protect — privacy, verification, moderation, consent, reporting and incident handling.

Huddle is not a social network for children. It is designed around adult-controlled family participation; children should not become public searchable profiles by default, and unknown adults should not contact children directly. `[C-015]` `[C-016]` `[C-017]`

## Public value by stakeholder

### Families

Huddle may help families:

- spend less time assembling information from many sources;
- compare activities by age, location, schedule, price, trust and accessibility;
- find free, subsidised, reused or lower-cost options;
- understand what is required before joining;
- make safer adult-controlled first contact;
- receive reminders and updates;
- return to useful local opportunities.

### Organisers

Huddle may help organisers:

- reach more relevant families;
- maintain clearer activity information;
- communicate capacity and age fit;
- reduce mismatched inquiries;
- build stronger trust signals;
- receive structured feedback;
- participate in measurable local pilots.

### Municipalities and public partners

Huddle may help public partners:

- improve visibility of existing programmes;
- identify local participation barriers;
- understand category demand;
- support inclusion initiatives;
- connect families to free or subsidised opportunities;
- receive aggregated, privacy-safe insight;
- evaluate a controlled pilot before broader procurement or partnership.

## Oslo city-cell validation

Huddle will start with one bounded Oslo city-cell before broader build-out. `[C-001]`

The first validation phase is designed to answer whether Huddle should proceed into MVP build and pilot preparation.

Planned validation targets:

- 40–60 parent/caregiver interviews; `[C-007]`
- 20–30 organiser and supply conversations; `[C-008]`
- 10–20 municipality, NGO or public-value partner conversations; `[C-009]`
- trust/privacy/safety model review;
- selection of one to three candidate Oslo neighbourhoods;
- MVP scope freeze;
- preliminary Trust & Safety and privacy baseline.

The day-90 decision should be:

- GO — evidence supports controlled MVP build and pilot preparation;
- ITERATE — meaningful signal exists, but segment, geography, scope or trust model must change;
- STOP-PAUSE — core assumptions are not strong enough to continue without a major rethink.

## Measurement framework

The pilot should measure:

- family problem severity and frequency;
- relevance of recommendations;
- trust and privacy friction;
- organiser data quality;
- progression from discovery to action;
- attendance and return signals;
- repeat participation;
- support requests and incidents;
- cost/equipment/accessibility barriers;
- public partner usefulness;
- organiser willingness to continue.

Huddle should not claim measured public impact before pilot evidence exists. `[C-020]`

## Privacy and child-safety boundary

The public-value model must remain privacy-first.

Potential public reporting should be aggregated and purpose-defined. It may include:

- activity supply by category;
- demand signals by neighbourhood;
- discovery-to-action flow;
- free/subsidised demand;
- equipment and cost barriers;
- accessibility barriers;
- repeat participation patterns;
- organiser data quality;
- support/incident categories at aggregate level.

It should not expose:

- individual child profiles;
- unrestricted family-level data;
- sensitive child or family dossiers;
- surveillance-style dashboards;
- unclear secondary use of family data.

Final privacy, child-safety and data-sharing wording requires human legal/privacy review before submission.

## Partnership request

Huddle is seeking early public-value partners for:

- pilot geography selection;
- introductions to relevant local programmes and organisers;
- validation input;
- privacy-safe measurement design;
- potential pilot co-funding;
- NGO/municipality partner-route discussions where relevant;
- future implementation learning.

The immediate goal is not broad deployment. It is a controlled Oslo validation and pilot path that produces decision-quality evidence.

## Evidence boundary

External statistics establish context. They do not prove demand for Huddle.

Huddle’s own claims must come from validation and pilot evidence. Before that, public materials should use:

- hypothesis;
- designed to;
- will test;
- planning assumption;
- pre-validation;
- controlled pilot;
- evidence-gated.
